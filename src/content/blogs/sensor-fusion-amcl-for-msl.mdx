# Integrating Encoder, IMU, and Vision for AMCL in MSL Robots

This write-up summarizes my research on improving AMCL localization for an MSL robot by combining three sensor sources: wheel encoder, IMU, and vision.

## Technical Theory

Before discussing experiments, the key theory is the AMCL particle filter itself and how multi-sensor fusion changes particle weighting.

### 1. AMCL as a Bayesian Estimator

AMCL estimates robot pose

$$
x_t = [x, y, \theta]_t
$$

with a set of particles:

$$
\{x_t^{(i)}, w_t^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^{N}
$$

At each time step, AMCL performs:

1. Prediction with motion model.
2. Measurement update with observation model.
3. Normalization and resampling.

The posterior is approximated as:

$$
p(x_t \mid z_{1:t}, u_{1:t}) \approx \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_t^{(i)} \delta(x_t - x_t^{(i)})
$$

### 2. Motion Model: Encoder + IMU

Wheel encoder gives incremental displacement and rotation from drivetrain kinematics. In practice this is smooth and high-rate, but drifts with slip and impact.

IMU contributes angular velocity and orientation stability, especially for fast turn transitions where wheel-only yaw can be noisy.

A simplified fused control increment can be written as:

$$
\Delta \hat{x}_t = [\Delta s, \Delta \theta]_t
$$

$$
\Delta \theta_t = (1-\beta)\Delta \theta_{enc} + \beta\Delta \theta_{imu}
$$

where $\beta$ controls IMU influence on heading update.

### 3. Measurement Model: Vision Landmarks

Vision provides global correction using field lines, goals, corners, and known landmarks. Unlike dead-reckoning, vision constrains absolute pose relative to the map.

Given expected measurement $\hat{z}^{(i)}$ from particle $x_t^{(i)}$ and actual observation $z_t$, the likelihood can be modeled as:

$$
p(z_t \mid x_t^{(i)}) \propto \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}(z_t-\hat{z}^{(i)})^T R^{-1}(z_t-\hat{z}^{(i)})\right)
$$

where $R$ is measurement covariance and effectively sets vision trust.

### 4. Particle Weight Update and Fusion Weights

In multi-sensor form, I split the update into modality-specific likelihood terms:

$$
w_t^{(i)} \propto
\left(p_{enc}^{(i)}\right)^{\lambda_{enc}}
\left(p_{imu}^{(i)}\right)^{\lambda_{imu}}
\left(p_{vis}^{(i)}\right)^{\lambda_{vis}}
$$

with

$$
\lambda_{enc}+\lambda_{imu}+\lambda_{vis}=1, \quad \lambda_k \ge 0
$$

This makes the weight search explicit: tune $(\lambda_{enc},\lambda_{imu},\lambda_{vis})$ for your field conditions.

### 5. Resampling Stability and Effective Sample Size

To avoid particle degeneracy, resampling is triggered from effective sample size:

$$
N_{eff} = \frac{1}{\sum_i (w_t^{(i)})^2}
$$

If $N_{eff}$ is too low, only a few particles dominate and recovery after collisions becomes poor. Good fusion weights should keep correction strong without collapsing diversity too aggressively.

## Experiment: Searching the Best AMCL Sensor Weights

### 1. Objective

Find fusion weights that minimize localization error while preserving stability during fast motion and contact events in MSL gameplay.

### 2. Setup

- Platform: MSL differential-drive robot.
- Sensors: wheel encoder, IMU, monocular vision landmarks.
- Map: official field geometry with known landmark positions.
- AMCL particles: fixed count across all trials.

### 3. Search Space

I used constrained grid search over fusion weights:

$$
\lambda_{enc},\lambda_{imu},\lambda_{vis} \in [0,1],
\quad \lambda_{enc}+\lambda_{imu}+\lambda_{vis}=1
$$

with step size 0.1 for initial scan, then a finer local sweep around top candidates.

### 4. Evaluation Metrics

For each weight triple, I logged:

- Mean translation error (m).
- Mean heading error (deg).
- Re-localization time after disturbance (s).
- Pose jump count (number of unstable corrections).

Composite score used for ranking:

$$
J = a\,E_{pos} + b\,E_{yaw} + c\,T_{relocalize} + d\,N_{jumps}
$$

with larger penalty on unstable jumps to prefer match-robust behavior.

### 5. Procedure

1. Reset AMCL and robot initial pose.
2. Run predefined trajectories: straight, arc turn, fast transition, and collision recovery.
3. Repeat runs for each weight triple.
4. Aggregate metrics across runs.
5. Select top candidates and run finer search around them.

### 6. Result Summary

A representative ranking from my experiments:

| $\lambda_{enc}$ | $\lambda_{imu}$ | $\lambda_{vis}$ | Qualitative behavior |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 0.30 | 0.20 | 0.50 | Best overall balance, fast correction with low jump rate |
| 0.40 | 0.20 | 0.40 | Good in smooth motion, weaker recovery after contact |
| 0.20 | 0.20 | 0.60 | Strong correction but occasional overreaction to noisy vision |
| 0.60 | 0.20 | 0.20 | Stable short-term, drift accumulates in long runs |

The best region in my logs favored moderate encoder trust, modest IMU contribution, and dominant but not extreme vision weight.

### 7. Analysis

- Too much encoder weight causes long-horizon drift.
- Too much vision weight causes correction spikes when visual confidence drops.
- IMU weight is most useful as heading stabilizer, not as dominant global correction source.

This supports the theory: encoder is local continuity, IMU is rotational damping, vision is global anchor.

## Practical Implementation Notes

- Use confidence gates for vision updates (feature count, reprojection quality, or landmark consistency).
- Increase process noise during high acceleration or slip-prone maneuvers.
- Keep a fallback mode when vision is unavailable, then smoothly restore vision weight when confidence returns.

## Conclusion

Integrating encoder, IMU, and vision in AMCL significantly improved localization robustness for MSL conditions, especially during high-speed transitions and post-collision recovery.

The main contribution of this experiment is not only finding one best weight triple, but establishing a repeatable search workflow that can be re-tuned for different lighting, field texture, and robot dynamics.

Next step is adaptive weighting, where $\lambda_{vis}$ and $\lambda_{imu}$ are adjusted online from confidence signals rather than fixed constants.
